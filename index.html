🗣️ The oral exam — 75 points, 3 parts, usually done in pairs

You normally speak with another candidate in front of two examiners (only if the number of candidates is odd is it done solo, with an examiner as your partner). The talk itself lasts at most 15 minutes per pair. Before it, you get 20 minutes of preparation time with the task sheets — you may write short notes, but in the exam you should talk, not read aloud.

How the 75 points are scored

Both examiners score you separately during the talk, then agree on a mark after you leave the room. Each part is rated on the same four things:

  • Ausdrucksfähigkeit — expression: can you say what you mean, with enough words?
  • Aufgabenbewältigung — doing the task: did you really discuss / plan / react to your partner?
  • Formale Richtigkeit — accuracy: grammar and sentence building.
  • Aussprache und Intonation — pronunciation and melody: easy to understand?
Good news: at B1 level, examiners expect mistakes. A wrong article costs almost nothing. Silence, or ignoring your partner, costs a lot. Keep talking, keep reacting — that is what "Aufgabenbewältigung" rewards.

✅ Pass rules — super simple

  • Written exam: 225 points possible → you need 135 (that is 60%).
  • Oral exam: 75 points possible → you need 45 (also 60%).
  • You must reach 60% in both parts. A great written score cannot rescue a failed oral, and the other way round.
  • Failed (or missed) only ONE part? You may repeat just that part — in the same calendar year or the next one. The part you passed stays valid.
  • The whole exam can be repeated as often as you want.
Memory trickThink "60 + 60": sixty percent written and sixty percent oral. Two separate doors — you must open both.

📖 Leseverstehen — how to beat each reading task

Teil 1 (headlines): read the text FIRST

Read one short text, say its main idea to yourself in your own words, and only then look for the headline that says the same idea. There are 10 headlines for 5 texts, so 5 headlines are traps.

The word trap: a wrong headline often repeats one word from the text. Same word ≠ same meaning. Match the idea, never a single word.

Teil 2 (long text): questions first, in order

Read the 5 questions before the text. The questions follow the text from top to bottom, so once you have found the answer to question 2, the answer to question 3 comes later in the text. Answer only from what the text says — not from your own knowledge or opinion.

Teil 3 (ads): every condition must fit

Underline the 2–3 conditions in each situation (what? when? how much?), then scan the 12 ads. An ad is only correct if all conditions fit. Each ad can be used only once — and if no ad fits a situation, you mark x on the answer sheet.

Situation: Sie suchen eine 2-Zimmer-Wohnung mit Balkon für maximal 600 Euro.Situation: You are looking for a 2-room flat with a balcony for a maximum of 600 euros.
Anzeige: Schöne 2-Zimmer-Wohnung mit Balkon, 650 Euro.Ad: Nice 2-room flat with balcony, 650 euros.
Balcony ✓, 2 rooms ✓ — but 650 > 600, so this ad is wrong. One broken condition kills the whole match. This is exactly how telc builds its traps.

Suggested time budget (inside the 90-minute block)

  • Lesen 1 — about 12 minutes
  • Lesen 2 — about 20 minutes
  • Lesen 3 — about 20 minutes
  • Sprachbausteine 1 + 2 — about 25 minutes
  • Copy answers to the Antwortbogen + final check — about 13 minutes

🧩 Sprachbausteine — 30 fast points if you know the patterns

Teil 1 tests grammar (10 gaps, 3 options each)

  • Connectors — weil vs. denn vs. deshalb vs. obwohl: the word order around the gap tells you the answer.
  • Prepositions — an, auf, für, mit, bei, von… mostly the fixed verb + preposition pairs.
  • Verb forms — würde/könnte/hätte, Perfekt forms, reflexive pronouns (mich, dich, sich).
  • Pronouns & articles — der/dem/den, relative pronouns, Ihnen vs. Sie.

Teil 2 tests vocabulary (10 gaps, choose from 15 words)

  • Fixed expressions and word partners: Vielen Dank für…, Ich freue mich auf…, typical letter phrases.
  • 15 words for 10 gaps → 5 words are never used. Fill the easy gaps first; every filled gap removes a word and makes the hard gaps easier.
  • Check what word type the gap needs: after "der/die/das" a noun, before a noun maybe an adjective, in position 2 a verb.
Method for every gap: read the whole sentence (often also the sentence before), decide what type of word is missing, then test each option quietly "by ear". Never answer a gap in isolation.
There is no penalty for wrong answers — so never, ever leave a gap empty. A blind guess is free points waiting to happen.

Answers — and why

GapAnswerWhy
1a) fürFixed pair: danken für — you always thank FOR something.
2c) weilThe verb "muss" sits at the end of the clause → that is "weil" word order. With "denn" the verb would come earlier: "…, denn ich muss arbeiten."

🎧 Hörverstehen — listening without panic

The golden rule: read the statements BEFORE the audio

The recording always gives you time to read the statements first — use every second of it. Underline the key word in each statement. While listening, you only check: does the audio say this, yes or no?

Teil 1 plays only ONCE. This is the hardest moment of the whole exam — there is no second chance. Train this part more than any other: full focus for those few minutes, phone away, one listen only when you practise.

Teil 2 (long conversation, plays twice)

The 10 statements follow the conversation in order. First listen: answer everything you can. Second listen: check and fix. If you missed one, mark a guess and let it go — richtig/falsch means even a blind guess wins half the time.

Teil 3 (announcements, plays twice): hunt the numbers

Announcements at stations, on answering machines, on the radio. The answer almost always hides in a number, time, price, date or platform — write these down the moment you hear them.

Der Zug nach Hamburg fährt heute um 14:30 Uhr von Gleis 7.The train to Hamburg leaves today at 2:30 pm from platform 7.
Never chase a lost answer. If you miss item 47, guess it and jump to item 48. Chasing one answer into the next text is how people lose three points instead of one.

Plan

Your 8-week plan (A2.1 to B1)

START HERE

Today is 14 July 2026. Your exam is in mid-September. That is about 8–9 weeks. With 1.5–2.5 focused hours a day, you can do this.

How to study with this guide

This guide is your whole course. The plan below tells you when to open which section. The method is simple and it works:

  • Grammar first (weeks 1–2). Grammar is the skeleton. Without Perfekt (the past tense for talking) and word order, you cannot build sentences — so we fix that first.
  • Vocabulary every single day — no exceptions. Use spaced repetition (a fancy name for a simple idea): every day you learn 10 new words, then you review yesterday’s words, then you review the words from the same day last week. Repeating at growing distances is what moves words into long-term memory.
  • Writing from week 3. The letter in the exam follows a fixed recipe. You will write at least one letter every week from week 3 on, so that on exam day it is routine, not stress.
  • Speaking out loud from week 4. Your mouth needs training, not only your eyes. Talk to yourself, to your mirror, to your phone’s voice recorder. Nobody is listening — but the muscles learn.
  • Mock timing from week 7. In the last two weeks you practise with a real clock. Knowing the answer is not enough; you must find it in the given time.

The “chunk” principle — the most important study rule

Never learn a word alone. Always learn it inside a short sentence (a “chunk”). A single word gives you nothing to say; a chunk gives you a ready-made piece of German. It also stores the article, the case and the preposition for free.

Not just “der Termin” — learn: Ich habe einen Termin beim Arzt.I have an appointment at the doctor’s.
Not just “warten” — learn: Ich warte auf den Bus.I am waiting for the bus.
Memory trickOne chunk = word + article + case + preposition, all in one package. Ten chunks a day beat fifty naked words a day.
You do not need perfect German for B1. You need about 60% of the points. Steady daily work beats talent — every single time.

Grammar 1

Grammar I – nouns, cases & adjectives

THE FOUNDATION

Cases, articles, pronouns, prepositions and adjective endings – the skeleton of every German sentence.

1. The four cases – what are they?

German nouns change their article depending on their job in the sentence. There are four jobs, called "cases". Don't panic: at B1 you really only need three of them well.

N Nominativthe subject – who does the actionWer? Was?
A Akkusativthe direct object – the thing the action happens toWen? Was?
D Dativthe receiver – to/for whom; also after Dativ prepositionsWem?
G Genitivpossession ("of the …") – rare at B1, mostly after wegen, während, trotzWessen?
Der Mann liest eine Zeitung.The man is reading a newspaper. (der Mann = subject = Nominativ)

Das ist das Auto des Mannes.That is the man's car. (Genitiv – rare, you mostly just need to recognise it)

Grammar II — the verb system

0. START HERE — the time machine 🕐 (what is a "tense"?)

A verb is a doing-word: trinken (to drink), machen (to make), gehen (to go). A "tense" is nothing scary — it just means the doing-word changes its clothes to show WHEN it happens. There are only three times in life:

⬅️ GESTERN (yesterday)🔴 JETZT (now)➡️ MORGEN (tomorrow)
Ich habe Kaffee getrunken.Ich trinke Kaffee.Morgen trinke ich Kaffee.
I drank coffee.I am drinking coffee.Tomorrow I'll drink coffee.
The best news in this whole guide: look at that table again. NOW and TOMORROW use the same word (trinke)! German needs only TWO verb forms for 95% of everything you will ever say: the now-form (Präsens) and the yesterday-form (Perfekt). That's it. Two forms. You can do this.

Rule 1 — NOW and TOMORROW: the now-form does both jobs

For anything happening now, use the now-form. For the future, use the same form + a time word (morgen, nächste Woche, am Samstag). No new grammar needed!

Ich lerne Deutsch.I am learning German. (now)
Morgen lerne ich Deutsch.Tomorrow I will learn German. (future = same verb + time word!)
Am Samstag besuche ich meine Freunde.On Saturday I will visit my friends.

Rule 2 — YESTERDAY: the sandwich trick 🥪

To say something already happened, German builds a sandwich. Two pieces of bread hold the sentence together, and everything else sits in the middle:

Who🍞 Helper (place 2)The middle🍞 ge-word (very end)
Ichhabegestern Kaffeegetrunken.
Wirhabeneine Pizzagemacht.
Ichbingestern nach Bonngefahren.

Baby recipe, three steps every single time:

  • Step 1: say who + the helper: Ich habe… (or Ich bin…)
  • Step 2: say everything else: when, what, where.
  • Step 3: throw the ge-word to the very END: …gemacht / gegessen / gefahren.
Ich habe am Wochenende meine Wohnung geputzt.I cleaned my flat at the weekend.
Er hat gestern einen Film gesehen.He watched a film yesterday.
Sie ist heute früh aufgestanden.She got up early today.
Which helper — habe or bin?Ask one baby question: "Did the person walk / travel / change?" 🚶 Moving or changing → bin/ist (Ich bin gefahren, er ist aufgestanden). Everything else → habe/hat (Ich habe gegessen, sie hat gekauft). Details in block 5.

Rule 3 — the three little cheats: war, hatte, wollte

For "was", "had" and "wanted/could/had to", Germans skip the sandwich and use one short word instead. Learn these three sentences and copy them forever:

Ich war gestern krank.I was ill yesterday. (NOT a sandwich — just "war")
Ich hatte keine Zeit.I had no time.
Ich musste arbeiten.I had to work. (also: wollte = wanted, konnte = could)

5. Perfekt — THE spoken past tense

When Germans talk about the past, they use Perfekt: haben/sein in position 2 + Partizip II at the end. In the letter and speaking parts, Perfekt is your default past tense.

Ich habe gestern eine Pizza gegessen.I ate a pizza yesterday.
Wir sind am Samstag nach Köln gefahren.We went to Cologne on Saturday.

haben or sein?

Use ist (sein) only for:

  • Movement from A to B: gehen, fahren, kommen, fliegen, laufen, steigen, umziehen → ist gegangen, ist gefahren
  • Change of state: aufstehen, einschlafen, aufwachen, sterben → ist aufgestanden, ist eingeschlafen
  • The special four: sein, bleiben, passieren, werden → ist gewesen, ist geblieben, ist passiert, ist geworden

Everything else takes hat: hat gemacht, hat gekauft, hat gesehen.

Memory trickAsk: "Did somebody or something move or change?" Yes → sein. No → haben. Plus learn "ist geblieben" as a weird exception — no movement, but still sein!

7. Plusquamperfekt — "the past before the past"

Built like Perfekt, but with hatte / war instead of habe / bin: hatte/war + Partizip II. You need it mainly after nachdem (after): the nachdem-part happened FIRST, so it gets Plusquamperfekt.

Nachdem ich die Prüfung bestanden hatte, habe ich mit Freunden gefeiert.After I had passed the exam, I celebrated with friends.
Nachdem wir angekommen waren, haben wir zuerst etwas gegessen.After we had arrived, we ate something first.
Simple recipe: take the Perfekt sentence, swap habe → hatte and bin → war. Done.

8. Futur I — talking about the future

Form: werden (position 2) + infinitive (end).

Ich werde nächstes Jahr nach Deutschland ziehen.I will move to Germany next year.
Es wird morgen bestimmt regnen.It will surely rain tomorrow. (werden + inf. also = guessing)
Real-life shortcut: Germans mostly use present tense + a time word for the future: Morgen fahre ich nach Berlin. (Tomorrow I'm going to Berlin.) That is perfect B1 German — use it in your letter and speaking.

9. Reflexive verbs — sich-verbs

Some verbs come with a mirror pronoun: sich freuen (to be happy). The pronoun changes with the person: ich → mich/mir, du → dich/dir, er/sie/es → sich, wir → uns, ihr → euch, sie/Sie → sich.

mich or mir? The body-part rule

Ich wasche mich.I wash myself. (no other object → Akkusativ "mich")
Ich wasche mir die Hände.I wash my hands. (there IS another object, "die Hände" → the pronoun switches to Dativ "mir")
Only ich and du show the difference (mich/mir, dich/dir). All the others look the same anyway (sich, uns, euch) — so this trap is small: just watch "mir/mich" and "dir/dich".

11. Passive — when the doer doesn't matter

Germans use the passive when the action is more important than who does it: rules, processes, news. Form: werden + Partizip II.

Präsens: Das Brot wird jeden Morgen frisch gebacken.The bread is baked fresh every morning.
Präteritum: Die Schule wurde 1985 gebaut.The school was built in 1985.
With a modal: Das Handy muss repariert werden.The phone has to be repaired. (modal + Partizip II + werden at the end)
The doer: Der Kuchen wird von meiner Mutter gebacken.The cake is baked by my mother. ("von" + Dativ = by whom)
You mostly need to understand passive in reading and listening (signs, instructions: "Hier wird gebaut", "Das Museum wird renoviert"). Producing 1–2 passive sentences is a bonus, not a must.

12. lassen — two very useful jobs

Job 1: have something done by someone else. lassen in position 2, infinitive at the end:

Job 2: make a suggestion — "Lass uns…" (let's…):

Lass uns am Samstag ins Kino gehen!Let's go to the cinema on Saturday! (perfect for speaking part 3: planning together)

14. Common verb traps — read this twice

wissen vs kennen: wissen = to know a FACT (Ich weiß, wo er wohnt). kennen = to know a PERSON, PLACE or THING (Ich kenne ihn / Ich kenne Berlin). Never "Ich weiß ihn".
können vs dürfen: können = ability (Ich kann schwimmen — I know how). dürfen = permission (Hier darf man nicht schwimmen — it's forbidden). Asking politely: "Darf ich hier sitzen?"
seit + PRESENT tense: If something started in the past and is STILL happening, German uses the present: Ich lerne seit zwei Jahren Deutsch. (I have been learning German for two years.) NOT "Ich habe seit zwei Jahren Deutsch gelernt."
gehen + infinitive (no zu): Ich gehe einkaufen. Wir gehen schwimmen. (I'm going shopping. We're going swimming.) No "zu", no comma.
es gibt + Akkusativ: the thing after "es gibt" is ALWAYS Akkusativ A: Es gibt einen Park / eine Schule / ein Kino. Also in the past: Es gab einen Unfall. Never "es gibt ein Park".
If you can use Perfekt with the right haben/sein, war/hatte, the modals, and "Könnten Sie / Ich hätte gern / Du solltest", your verb grammar is already strong enough to pass B1. Everything else here is polish.
Grammar 3

Grammar III — building sentences

WORD ORDER = POINTS

Where does the verb go? That is 80% of German word order. Master the 3 rules and the 3 connector buckets here, and your letter and speaking jump a whole level.

1. The Position-2 rule — the verb is ALWAYS the 2nd idea

In a normal German statement, the conjugated verb (the verb form that changes with the person: ich gehe, du gehst) sits in position 2. Not always the second word — the second idea. "Am Montag" is three words but ONE idea.

Ich gehe heute ins Kino.I am going to the cinema today.
Heute gehe ich ins Kino.Today I am going to the cinema.
Am Montag habe ich einen Termin.On Monday I have an appointment.
Leider kann ich nicht kommen.Unfortunately I cannot come.

Inversion — the subject flips

If something else takes position 1 (a time word, "leider", a whole clause…), the subject moves to position 3, right behind the verb. This flip is called Inversion. The verb never moves from position 2.

Most common A2 mistake: "Heute ich gehe ins Kino." ✗ — the verb got pushed to position 3. Correct: Heute gehe ich ins Kino. ✓ Start a sentence with a time word in the exam? Flip the subject!
Memory trickThe verb is a VIP with a fixed seat: seat number 2. Whoever sits in seat 1 — ich, heute, leider, am Montag — the verb never gives up seat 2. If seat 1 is taken by something new, the subject just moves to seat 3.

2. Questions — verb 2nd or verb 1st

W-questions (wer, was, wo, wann, warum, wie…) — verb in position 2

Wo wohnst du?Where do you live?
Wann beginnt der Kurs?When does the course start?

Yes/no questions — verb in position 1

Kommst du morgen zur Party?Are you coming to the party tomorrow?
Können Sie mir bitte helfen?Can you please help me?
Rule of thumb: question word first → verb 2nd. No question word → verb jumps to the front.

3. The sentence bracket (Satzklammer)

Very often a sentence has TWO verb parts. Part 1 (the conjugated verb) sits in position 2. Part 2 (participle, infinitive, or separable prefix) goes to the very END. Together they form a bracket around everything else — Germans call it the Satzklammer (sentence bracket).

Position 1Verb (Pos. 2)MiddleEND
Ichhabegestern Pizzagegessen.
Ichmussmorgen frühaufstehen.
Ichrufedich heute Abendan.
Ichwerdeim September die Prüfungmachen.

Row by row: Perfekt (I ate pizza yesterday) — modal verb (I have to get up early tomorrow) — separable verb (I'll call you tonight) — Futur with werden (I will take the exam in September).

The second verb part comes LAST — after time, after place, after everything. "Ich habe gegessen gestern Pizza." ✗ → Ich habe gestern Pizza gegessen. ✓

4. The middle of the sentence: TeKaMoLo

What order do the details go in, inside the bracket? Rule: Te-Ka-Mo-Lo = Temporal (when?) → Kausal (why?) → Modal (how? with what?) → Lokal (where? where to?).

Ich fahre morgen wegen eines Termins mit dem Zug nach Berlin.I am travelling to Berlin by train tomorrow because of an appointment. (when → why → how → where)
Sie fährt jeden Tag mit dem Bus zur Arbeit.She goes to work by bus every day. (when → how → where)
Wir waren gestern mit Freunden im Kino.We were at the cinema with friends yesterday. (when → with whom → where)
Memory trickCan't remember four things? Remember ONE: time before place. English says "I'm going to Berlin tomorrow", German says the opposite: Ich fahre morgen nach Berlin. This alone fixes most mistakes.

6. Worked comparison: weil vs denn vs deshalb

Same idea, three ways. Watch only the verb.

Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Deutschland arbeiten möchte.I'm learning German because I want to work in Germany. (weil → verb at the END)
Ich lerne Deutsch, denn ich möchte in Deutschland arbeiten.I'm learning German, because I want to work in Germany. (denn → normal word order)
Ich möchte in Deutschland arbeiten, deshalb lerne ich Deutsch.I want to work in Germany, that's why I'm learning German. (deshalb → verb right after; note: reason comes FIRST)
With deshalb the sentence flips direction: reason first, result second. Don't just swap weil for deshalb in the same slot — that's a classic trap in Sprachbausteine.

What piles up at the end?

In a Nebensatz, ALL verb parts move to the end. Order: the other parts first, the conjugated verb LAST.

…, weil ich ihn anrufen muss.…because I have to call him. (infinitive + modal — modal last)
…, weil ich zu spät aufgestanden bin.…because I got up too late. (participle + ist/bin — auxiliary last)
…, weil ich morgen früh aufstehe.…because I get up early tomorrow. (separable verb joins back together: auf + stehe → aufstehe!)
A separable verb (aufstehen, anrufen, einkaufen…) splits in a main clause — Ich stehe früh auf — but at the end of a Nebensatz it GLUES back together: …, weil ich früh aufstehe. Never write "…, weil ich früh stehe auf." ✗

9. Relative clauses (Relativsätze) — describe things like a pro

A relative clause adds information about a noun: "the man who lives next door". It is a Nebensatz → verb at the end, commas around it. The relative pronoun looks like "der/die/das", except one form: denen.

How to choose the pronoun

Gender and number come from the noun BEFORE the clause; the case comes from the pronoun's job INSIDE the clause.

Der Mann, der dort steht, ist mein Lehrer.The man who is standing there is my teacher. (der = subject inside → Nominativ)
Das Buch, das ich gerade lese, ist spannend.The book that I'm reading right now is exciting. (das = the thing being read → Akkusativ)
Die Frau, der ich geholfen habe, ist meine Nachbarin.The woman whom I helped is my neighbour. (helfen takes Dativ → der)
Der Mann, mit dem ich arbeite, kommt aus Italien.The man I work with comes from Italy. (preposition moves in FRONT of the pronoun; mit takes Dativ)
Die Freunde, mit denen ich wohne, sind sehr nett.The friends I live with are very nice. (plural Dativ → denen)
Das ist die Stadt, wo ich geboren bin.That is the city where I was born. (for places you can simply use "wo")
Comma rules: a comma BEFORE the relative clause, and another AFTER it if the sentence continues. Der Mann, der dort steht, ist mein Lehrer.

10. Indirect questions — polite exam gold

Wrap a question inside a polite sentence: W-word (or "ob" for yes/no questions) + verb at the END. This makes letters and the speaking exam instantly more polite — examiners love it.

Wo ist der Bahnhof? → Können Sie mir sagen, wo der Bahnhof ist?Where is the station? → Can you tell me where the station is?
Kommt er morgen? → Ich weiß nicht, ob er morgen kommt.Is he coming tomorrow? → I don't know whether he's coming tomorrow.
Ich möchte wissen, wann der Kurs beginnt.I would like to know when the course starts.

  • Könnten Sie mir bitte mitteilen, ob …? — Could you please let me know whether …?
  • Ich möchte gern wissen, ob … — I would like to know whether …
  • Ich frage mich, ob … — I wonder whether …
  • Wissen Sie, ob …? — Do you know whether …?
Yes/no question inside? Use ob, never "wenn": Ich weiß nicht, ob er kommt. ✓ — "wenn" here means "if/whenever" and changes the meaning.

12. Sentence upgrades: from A2 to B1 in one move

Examiners give points for CONNECTED sentences. Take two short A2 sentences and glue them with one tool from this section. Five before/after pairs — steal these patterns for your letter and your speaking test.

① Add a weil-clause

A2: Ich bin müde. Ich gehe schlafen.I'm tired. I'm going to sleep.
B1: Ich gehe schlafen, weil ich sehr müde bin.I'm going to sleep because I'm very tired.

② Add a relative clause

A2: Ich habe eine Wohnung. Die Wohnung ist klein.I have a flat. The flat is small.
B1: Ich habe eine Wohnung, die leider sehr klein ist.I have a flat which unfortunately is very small.

③ Add Konjunktiv II (the polite "would/could" form)

A2: Ich will mein Geld zurück.I want my money back.
B1: Könnten Sie mir bitte das Geld zurückzahlen? Ich wäre Ihnen sehr dankbar.Could you please refund me the money? I would be very grateful to you.

④ Add obwohl / trotzdem

A2: Der Kurs war teuer. Er war nicht gut.The course was expensive. It was not good.
B1: Obwohl der Kurs teuer war, war er leider nicht gut.Although the course was expensive, it unfortunately was not good.

⑤ Add a two-part connector

A2: Das Zimmer war laut. Das Essen war schlecht.The room was noisy. The food was bad.
B1: Nicht nur das Zimmer war laut, sondern auch das Essen war schlecht.Not only was the room noisy, but the food was also bad.
One upgraded sentence per paragraph is enough. Three weil/obwohl/relative clauses in your letter + one Könnten-Sie question = a visibly B1 text. You don't need fancy words — you need connected sentences.

  • Perfekt = the spoken yesterday form — the one from the sandwich trick 🥪 in Grammar II, block 0: helper (hat or ist) + ge-word at the end. THIS is the column to memorise, together with the Infinitiv: "trinken → hat getrunken".
Ich trinke jeden Morgen Tee. Gestern habe ich Kaffee getrunken.I drink tea every morning. Yesterday I drank coffee. — one verb, now-form and yesterday-form.

What does "irregular" mean? Obedient verbs vs rule-breaker verbs

Regular (obedient) verbs follow one simple recipe for the ge-word, no surprises: ge + stem + t.

machen → gemacht · kaufen → gekauft · lernen → gelerntmade · bought · learned — the recipe works every time. Nothing to memorise!

Irregular (rule-breaker) verbs refuse the recipe. Instead, the vowel inside the word changes (a vowel = the singing letters a, e, i, o, u):

trinken → getrunken (not "getrinkt"!) · gehen → gegangen · nehmen → genommendrunk · gone · taken — the inside of the word changes, so each one must be learned.

Why do these exist? They are the oldest, most-used words in the language — so old that they kept their ancient forms while younger verbs adopted the recipe. English does exactly the same: you say "go → went" (not "goed") and "eat → ate" (not "eated"). Annoying — but it also means every irregular verb you learn is a word you will use constantly. That's why the table says "Top 60": they ARE daily German.

🧲 Verbs with fixed prepositions 61 combos — Sprachbausteine gold

First, what is a preposition? One of the tiny direction words: auf, an, mit, von, für, über, um… German has "married" certain verbs to certain tiny words forever: warten + auf (to wait FOR), träumen + von (to dream OF). There is no logic to which word — English does the same thing ("depend ON", "afraid OF") — so never learn "warten" alone, learn "warten auf" as ONE word. These combos are tested directly in Sprachbausteine (the gap-fill part). The case chip shows which form the next word takes: A = Akkusativ (the case for the thing the action happens to) · D = Dativ (the "to/for whom" case).

Ich warte auf den Bus. · Sie träumt von einem Urlaub.I'm waiting for the bus. · She dreams of a holiday.

How to learn vocabulary with this guide

1. The gender colour code

Every noun has a colour chip: der = blue, die = red, das = green. Build a picture in your head: imagine der-words frozen ice blue, die-words burning fire red, das-words grass green. Example: der Vater is a father frozen in blue ice. Silly pictures stick!

⚠️ One important exception: in the plural, every German noun uses "die" — even masculine and neuter ones: der Tisch → die Tische, das Buch → die Bücher. This plural "die" is not the feminine article and has no colour meaning. Words that exist only in the plural ("nur Plural") therefore get a grey chip: die Eltern, die Geschwister. Grey = there is no gender to memorise here — just learn the word with "die". Meine Eltern wohnen in Berlin. — My parents live in Berlin.

2. Always learn the plural together

Never learn "Kind". Learn das Kind, -er → die Kinder. The plural ending stands after the comma in every table. "Mütter" means the vowel changes: die Mutter → die Mütter.

3. The 3-step daily method (15 minutes)

  • Read one theme out loud, German + English.
  • Cover the English column with your hand and test yourself. Mark the words you forgot.
  • Use 5 of the words in your own spoken sentences. Say them out loud — speaking is part of the exam!

Careful: rosa, lila and orange never change their ending: „eine rosa Mütze", „ein lila Schal" — no -e, no -en. All other colours take normal adjective endings: „ein rotes Kleid", „die gelbe Jacke". After „in" you write the colour with a capital letter: „Haben Sie das Hemd in Blau?" (Do you have the shirt in blue?)
Memory trickBuild any shade with hell- (light) and dunkel- (dark) glued to the front: hell + blau = hellblau (light blue), dunkel + rot = dunkelrot (dark red). Two prefixes, one rule — and you can describe every colour on the Sprechen picture card.
Careful: die Hose is singular in German! One pair of trousers = eine Hose. So: „Die Hose ist neu." (The trousers are new.) The same works for die Brille (glasses) and die Schere (scissors).
Memory trickGerman words are LEGO: long words are just small words clicked together. Kühl + Schrank = cold + cupboard = fridge. Wasch + Maschine = wash + machine. Hand + Schuh = hand + shoe = glove! Learn the small parts and you can guess hundreds of words. Bonus rule: the last part decides the gender — der Schrank, so it is also der Kühlschrank; die Karte, so it is die Speisekarte.
Memory trickFood gender hints: most fruits are die (die Banane, die Birne, die Erdbeere, die Zitrone, die Orange, die Traube) — the big exception is der Apfel. Most alcoholic drinks are der (der Wein, der Schnaps) — the big exception is das Bier. And words ending in -e are usually die (die Suppe, die Torte, die Gurke, die Tomate).

Writing — the letter (45 points)

HIGHEST ROI

One semi-formal letter or e-mail, 30 minutes, fully formulaic. Learn the skeleton + phrases here and these 45 points are almost a gift.

The task

You get a short situation (for example: "Your German course was bad. Write to the language school.") plus 4 Leitpunkte — guiding points, small bullet points that tell you what your letter must talk about. You write one reply letter in about 30 minutes. Aim for 80–120 words — more is not better, correct and complete is better.

How it is graded (in simple words)

  • Content — did you answer all 4 guiding points?
  • Structure — greeting, clear middle part, closing line, sign-off. Does it look like a real letter?
  • Language — grammar, spelling, word order. Small mistakes are OK at B1. The examiners expect them.

All three criteria count equally — 15 points each.

The killer rule: missing one Leitpunkt = a massive point loss. And telc's official rule: if you properly cover only one Leitpunkt or none — or your language has so many mistakes that the letter is barely understandable — the whole letter scores 0 points, even if everything else is fine. So: write the 4 points on your paper, and tick each one off while you write. Never hand in a letter with an unticked point.
Good news: nobody expects beautiful German. A simple letter that covers all 4 points with the fixed phrases below beats a "creative" letter that forgets one point. Boring wins here.

The skeleton (never change the order)

  1. Anrede (greeting) + comma → next line starts lowercase
  2. Einleitung (opening) — why you are writing (1 sentence)
  3. Hauptteil (main part) — answer every Leitpunkt, each one with its own connector word
  4. Schluss (closing) — a final line: a request, a thank-you, or a hope
  5. Grußformel (sign-off) + your name on the next line

Two tiny rules that examiners always check

Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren,
ich schreibe Ihnen, weil …After the greeting comes a comma — and the next line continues with a lowercase letter, because the sentence is not finished yet.
du or Sie — decide first, stay consistent. The task tells you who you write to. Friend → du / dich / dir / dein. Company, landlord, boss, stranger → Sie / Ihnen / Ihr. Mixing them ("Liebe Anna, … ich danke Ihnen") costs points every single time. Underline "friend" or "formal" in the task before you write one word.

Anrede (greeting)
  • Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren, — formal, unknown recipient ("Dear Sir or Madam")
  • Sehr geehrter Herr Müller, / Sehr geehrte Frau Müller, — formal, named person
  • Liebe Anna, / Lieber Tom, — friend / familiar person (du). "Liebe" for women, "Lieber" for men!
Einleitung (opening)
  • Ich schreibe Ihnen, weil … — I'm writing to you because…
  • Vielen Dank für Ihre E-Mail / Ihr Angebot. — Thanks for your email / your offer.
  • Ich habe Ihre Anzeige gelesen und interessiere mich für … — I read your ad and I'm interested in…
  • Wie geht es dir? Ich hoffe, dir geht es gut. — How are you? I hope you're well. (informal opener)
  • Es tut mir leid, dass ich mich so lange nicht gemeldet habe. — Sorry that I haven't been in touch for so long.
Hauptteil — connecting your points
  • Zuerst möchte ich sagen, dass … — First I'd like to say that…
  • Außerdem … / Darüber hinaus … — Furthermore… / On top of that…
  • Ein weiterer Punkt ist, dass … — Another point is that…
  • Der Grund dafür ist, dass … — The reason for that is that…
  • Ich würde gern wissen, ob … — I'd like to know whether…
  • Könnten Sie mir bitte sagen, …? — Could you please tell me…?
  • Leider … / Zum Glück … — Unfortunately… / Luckily…
  • Einerseits … , andererseits … — On the one hand… on the other hand… (perfect for pros and cons)
Making requests / suggestions
  • Ich möchte Sie bitten, … zu … — I'd like to ask you to…
  • Wäre es möglich, …? — Would it be possible to…?
  • Ich schlage vor, dass wir … — I suggest that we…
  • Ich hätte gern … / Ich würde gern … — I'd like… / I'd like to…
  • Ich bitte Sie um Verständnis. — I ask for your understanding.
Apologising / complaining
  • Es tut mir (sehr) leid, dass … — I'm (very) sorry that…
  • Ich möchte mich entschuldigen. — I would like to apologise.
  • Leider muss ich mich beschweren. — Unfortunately I have to complain.
  • Ich bin damit nicht zufrieden. — I'm not happy with that.
Schluss + Grußformel (closing)
  • Ich freue mich auf Ihre Antwort. — I look forward to your reply.
  • Vielen Dank im Voraus. — Thanks in advance.
  • Über eine baldige Rückmeldung würde ich mich freuen. — I'd be happy to hear back from you soon.
  • Schreib mir bald! / Bis bald! — Write me soon! / See you soon! (informal)
  • Mit freundlichen Grüßen — formal sign-off (your name on the next line)
  • Viele Grüße / Liebe Grüße — informal sign-off
Memory trick — the "connector sandwich"Start each Hauptteil point with a different connector: Zuerst… → Außerdem… → Ein weiterer Punkt ist… → Zum Schluss… This alone makes your letter look organised and B1-level, and it forces you to cover all four Leitpunkte. Examiners reward visible structure.

The 10 mistakes that cost the most points

  1. Lowercase nouns. Every German noun starts with a capital letter: die Wohnung, der Termin, das Problem. Examiners see this instantly.
  2. Verb not in position 2. In a normal sentence the verb is always the 2nd idea: Gestern habe ich … — not Gestern ich habe …
  3. Verb not at the end after weil / dass / wenn. …, weil ich am Wochenende arbeiten muss. The verb goes to the very end.
  4. No comma before dass / weil / wenn / ob. Ich hoffe, dass du Zeit hast.
  5. Sie / Ihnen / Ihr lowercase in a formal letter. In formal letters the polite "you" is ALWAYS capitalised: Ich danke Ihnen für Ihre E-Mail.
  6. No Anrede + comma. A letter without a greeting line and its comma loses structure points before the examiner reads one sentence.
  7. Mixing du and Sie in the same letter. Decide once, check every "you" word at the end.
  8. Forgetting a Leitpunkt. The single biggest point-killer. Tick all 4 off on the exam paper.
  9. English word order. Ich habe gekauft gestern ein Buch ✗ → Ich habe gestern ein Buch gekauft ✓. The second verb part goes to the end.
  10. Writing too little or no sign-off. Under ~80 words = content can't be complete. And always finish with Mit freundlichen Grüßen (or Viele Grüße) plus your name.

Practice method — from copying to exam speed

Weeks 1–2 — copy and absorb
  • Pick 2 model letters per week. Copy each one by hand, slowly. Your hand learns the skeleton, the commas, the capitals.
  • Say the bold phrases out loud while copying — they must become automatic.
Weeks 3–5 — write without looking
  • Read only the situation + Leitpunkte of a model letter, close the page, and write your own answer.
  • Then compare with the model. Mark every difference — that's your personal mistake list.
  • Write at least 2 letters per week. Change small details (names, dates, reasons) so it stays fresh.
Weeks 6–8 — exam conditions
  • New task, 30-minute timer, no notes, no dictionary. One letter every 2–3 days.
  • Always keep 3–4 minutes at the end for the self-check below.

Self-check after EVERY letter (tap to tick)

  • Did I answer all 4 Leitpunkte?
  • Anrede with a comma, next line lowercase?
  • Is the verb in position 2 in every main sentence?
  • Verb at the end after weil / dass / wenn?
  • du or Sie — 100% consistent (and Sie/Ihnen/Ihr capitalised)?
  • At least 80 words?
  • Sign-off + my name at the end?
Do this method and by mid-September the letter will feel like filling in a form. That's the goal: no creativity under stress — just your trained skeleton with new details.
Speak

Speaking — all 3 parts with full dialogues (75 points)

HIGHEST ROI

The oral exam is 75 points and runs almost entirely on set phrases. Learn them until they are automatic — then even under stress your mouth knows what to say.

How the oral exam works

You normally do the speaking exam in pairs — you and one other candidate (only if the number of candidates is odd is it done solo, with one examiner as your conversation partner). Before it starts, you get 20 minutes of preparation time in a separate room. You may make notes there (keywords, not full sentences!), but no dictionary is allowed. Then you go in front of two examiners for about 15 minutes. One examiner leads the conversation, the other one listens and takes notes.

PartWhat happensPoints
Teil 1 — Kontaktaufnahme (3–4 min)You introduce yourselves and ask each other personal questions15
Teil 2 — Gespräch über ein Thema (5–6 min)Each of you presents a short topic, then you discuss it together30
Teil 3 — Gemeinsam eine Aufgabe lösen (5–6 min)You plan something together and reach a decision30

What the examiners really grade

  • Can you communicate? Does the message arrive — even with small mistakes?
  • Interaction! Do you react to your partner, ask back, keep the conversation alive?
  • Vocabulary and phrases — do you have words for the situation?
  • Grammar and pronunciation — they only count if mistakes make you hard to understand.
Golden rule: it is a conversation, not a monologue. React to your partner ("Ach, wirklich?"), then ask something back ("Und du?"). Two people talking WITH each other score higher than two people giving perfect speeches NEXT to each other.
Mistakes are OK. "Ich habe gegangen" is wrong, but the examiner understands you — you still get your communication points. Silence gets zero points. Keep talking.

Teil 1 — Introduce yourself (Kontaktaufnahme)

Prepare a ~90-second self-intro you can say in your sleep. Cover: name, origin, where you live, job/study, family, hobbies, languages, why you learn German.

Building blocks
  • Ich heiße … und ich komme aus … — My name is … and I come from …
  • Ich wohne seit … Jahren in … — I've lived in … for … years
  • Von Beruf bin ich … / Ich arbeite als … / Ich studiere … — By profession I am … / I work as … / I study …
  • In meiner Freizeit … (spiele ich Fußball / lese ich gern / treffe ich Freunde). — In my free time … (I play football / I like reading / I meet friends).
  • Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Deutschland leben/arbeiten möchte. — I'm learning German because I want to live/work in Germany.
  • Ich spreche … und ein bisschen Deutsch. — I speak … and a little German.
  • Und du? / Und Sie? Was machst du beruflich? — And you? What do you do for a living? (turn it back to your partner!)

Teil 2 — Talk about a topic (official name: Gespräch über ein Thema)

The real format: in the preparation room, you and your partner each get a short text or topic sheet (both about the same theme, e.g. two people's opinions about smartphones). In the exam you first tell your partner in 2–3 sentences what your text says, then you give your own opinion — and then you DISCUSS the topic together. The discussion is the important part, not the little presentation.

The 5-step formula — works for ANY topic

  • 1. Say the topic: "In meinem Text geht es um …" — My text is about …
  • 2. Your opinion: "Ich finde, dass …" — I think that …
  • 3. Your experience: "Bei mir ist es so: …" — In my case, it's like this: …
  • 4. One pro + one contra: "Einerseits … andererseits …" — On one hand … on the other …
  • 5. Ask your partner: "Und du? Wie ist das bei dir?" — And you? How is it for you?
Giving your opinion
  • Meiner Meinung nach … — In my opinion …
  • Ich finde / Ich glaube, dass … — I think / I believe that …
  • Ich bin der Meinung, dass … — I am of the opinion that …
  • Für mich ist es wichtig, dass … — For me it's important that …
Examples & experience
  • Zum Beispiel … / Ein Beispiel dafür ist … — For example … / An example of that is …
  • Bei mir ist es so, dass … — In my case …
  • Ich habe die Erfahrung gemacht, dass … — I've experienced that …
  • Als ich … war, … — When I was …
Weighing pros & cons
  • Einerseits … andererseits … — On one hand … on the other …
  • Der Vorteil ist … / Der Nachteil ist … — The advantage is … / The disadvantage is …
  • Das kommt darauf an. — It depends.
  • Trotzdem … / Obwohl … — Nevertheless … / Although …
Reacting to your partner (graded!)
  • Da stimme ich dir zu. — I agree with you.
  • Da hast du recht, aber … — You're right, but …
  • Das sehe ich (ganz) anders. — I see that (quite) differently.
  • Wirklich? Das ist interessant! — Really? That's interesting!
  • Was meinst du dazu? — What do you think about that?

Teil 3 — Plan something together (official name: Gemeinsam eine Aufgabe lösen)

The interaction test. You + your partner organise something (party, trip, gift, visit …). Suggest → react → agree → decide. This part is a phrase machine — feed it.

Making a suggestion
  • Ich schlage vor, dass wir … — I suggest we …
  • Wir könnten … / Wollen wir …? — We could … / Shall we …?
  • Was hältst du von …? / Wie wäre es mit …? — What about …?
  • Hast du eine Idee? / Was meinst du? — Do you have an idea? / What do you think?
Agreeing
  • Das ist eine gute Idee! — That's a good idea!
  • Ja, das finde ich auch. / Da stimme ich dir zu. — Yes, I think so too. / I agree with you.
  • Einverstanden! / Gut, machen wir das. — Agreed! / OK, let's do that.
Disagreeing politely + counter-proposal
  • Das sehe ich anders. / Ich bin nicht sicher, ob … — I see that differently. / I'm not sure whether …
  • Das ist eine gute Idee, aber … — That's a good idea, but …
  • Vielleicht ist es besser, wenn wir … / Wäre es nicht besser, …? — Maybe it's better if we … / Wouldn't it be better to …?
Deciding & wrapping up
  • Dann machen wir es so. / Also, wir treffen uns um … — Then let's do it like that. / So, we'll meet at …
  • Wer kümmert sich um …? — Ich übernehme das. — Who'll take care of …? — I'll do that.
  • Ich kümmere mich um … / Du bringst … mit. — I'll take care of … / You bring … along.
  • Abgemacht! / Perfekt, dann sind wir fertig. — Deal! / Perfect, then we're done.

The WH-checklist — work through it and you never run dry

Whatever you have to plan, these six questions ARE the conversation. Tick them off in your head, one by one.

  • Was? — What exactly do we do?
  • Wann? — When? (day + time)
  • Wo? — Where? (place + how to get there)
  • Wer kommt? — Who comes / whom do we invite?
  • Was bringen wir mit? — What do we bring? (food, drinks, gift …)
  • Wer organisiert was? — Who organises what? (split the tasks!)
Exam hack — the ping-pongNever accept the first idea silently. Do a mini ping-pong: partner suggests → you react ("Gute Idee, aber…") → you counter → you agree. Three exchanges per decision shows exactly the interaction examiners score. Slower and interactive beats fast and solo.

Rescue phrases — when you're stuck

Every candidate gets stuck sometimes. The difference between passing and failing is what you do in that second. These phrases save you.

Didn't understand?
  • Wie bitte? — Pardon?
  • Entschuldigung, können Sie das bitte wiederholen? — Sorry, can you please repeat that? (to the examiner)
  • Kannst du das bitte wiederholen? — Can you please repeat that? (to your partner)
  • Können Sie bitte etwas langsamer sprechen? — Can you please speak a bit more slowly?
  • Habe ich das richtig verstanden? Du meinst …? — Did I understand correctly? You mean …?
Need time or a word?
  • Moment, lass mich kurz überlegen. — One moment, let me think for a second.
  • Wie sagt man … auf Deutsch? — How do you say … in German?
  • Ich weiß nicht, wie das Wort heißt, aber ich meine … — I don't know the word, but I mean … (then describe it!)
  • Ich meine … / Ich wollte sagen … — I mean … / I wanted to say … (self-correction is a GOOD sign)
  • Das ist eine gute Frage … — That's a good question … (classic time-buyer)
Asking for repetition costs NO points. Silence does. "Wie bitte?" is a normal part of real German conversation — examiners hear it every day and don't mind at all.

Memory trickOnly the masculine changes in Akkusativ (the case for the thing the action happens to): der → den, ein → einen. Everything else stays like Nominativ.

haben or sein in the Perfekt (past tense)?

ist only for movement A→B (gehen, fahren, fliegen, kommen) and change of state (aufstehen, einschlafen) plus sein, bleiben, passieren. Everything else: hat.

Ich bin nach Berlin gefahren und habe meine Tante besucht.I went to Berlin and visited my aunt.

The 3 connector buckets (same sentence, three ways)

  • Bucket 1 — nothing changes (und, denn): Ich bleibe zu Hause, denn ich bin krank. — I stay home, because I am sick.
  • Bucket 2 — verb goes to the END (weil, dass): Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich krank bin. — I stay home because I am sick.
  • Bucket 3 — verb comes right after (deshalb, trotzdem): Ich bin krank, deshalb bleibe ich zu Hause. — I am sick, that's why I stay home.

2. Letter skeleton — 5 lines, every time

  • 1. Greeting: formal Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren, (Dear Sir or Madam,) / informal Liebe Anna, (Dear Anna,) — then a comma and a small first letter on the next line.
  • 2. Opening line: vielen Dank für Ihre E-Mail. — thank you very much for your e-mail.
  • 3. Main part (all the given points!): Ich schreibe Ihnen, weil ich eine Frage zu Ihrem Kurs habe. — I am writing to you because I have a question about your course.
  • 4. Closing line: Ich freue mich auf Ihre Antwort. — I look forward to your reply.
  • 5. Sign-off + name: formal Mit freundlichen Grüßen (Kind regards) / informal Viele Grüße (Best wishes) — no comma, then your name.

30-second self-check before you hand it in

  • Did I answer ALL given points (Leitpunkte)? Count them!
  • du OR Sie — the same one everywhere?
  • Greeting at the top, sign-off + name at the bottom?
  • Verb in 2nd position — and at the END after weil / dass / wenn?
  • Every noun starts with a CAPITAL letter?
  • Did I use at least one connector (weil, deshalb, trotzdem)?

4. The 8 traps that fail candidates

Trap 1 — forgetting a Leitpunkt. Miss one of the given points in the letter and you lose a big block of points. Count the points before AND after writing.
Trap 2 — mixing du and Sie. Formal letter = Sie/Ihnen/Ihre everywhere. Friend = du/dir/deine everywhere. Mixing them costs points every time.
Trap 3 — verb not at the end after weil / dass. "...weil ich bin krank" is wrong. Say it slowly: ..., weil ich krank bin.
Trap 4 — nouns without a capital letter. Every German noun gets a capital: die Wohnung, der Termin, das Problem. Check this in your last 30 seconds.
Trap 5 — Hören Teil 1 plays only ONCE. Read the questions BEFORE the audio starts. If you read while listening, the track is gone.
Trap 6 — empty gaps in Sprachbausteine (the grammar part). There is NO penalty for wrong answers. Never leave a gap empty — always guess.
Trap 7 — a monologue in Sprechen Teil 3. It is a PLANNING TALK, not a speech. Ask your partner questions, react, agree, disagree — the examiners score interaction.
Trap 8 — panicking over one unknown word. Skip it. Every task gives new, independent points. One lost word never fails you — lost time does.
